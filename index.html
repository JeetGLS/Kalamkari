Welcome To Kalamkari

Everyone in the family used to stay here at Khanpur itself but since the localities have changed. “We are the only family doing this craft these days, there's no one else,'' says Satishbhai Chitara. This is a local craft. The local people that buy these stuff are generally the Rabaris and Thakors, they find it for cheap as it is block printed and not hand painted. They cannot afford the complete artwork handmade and hand-painted because the cost of handmade art is more than the block printed. The beginning as in the villages traditionally, but now this craft is recognised as antiquity and a collectable. Before this art was made by hand and eventually block printed by mud blocks and wooden blocks to suffice the demand for affordable artworks. And now again since people have started to recognise this craft and are now willing to pay for the efforts that they put in, they have again started to make it completely hand made.

Why Choose Us

The Heritage

They love the culture. They are ready to pay anything to learn the craft and buy the pieces. “Our craft is valued more by the westerners than our own countrymen,” says Satishbhai.

Colors

First, they have to dye the cloth with harde powder(Terminalia) and the process is called harde dyeing. After dyeing, colours can be used like black colour or red colour. The black colour is made using iron rust and jaggery mixed together and is left to ferment for 15 - 20days, a chemical process takes place in the mixture. Thereafter ambli seed (tamarind) powder called as kachka powder is mixed and boiled to make the black colour.

Brushes

All the handwork is done using paintbrush these days.But in the olden days, brushes were made from twigs of date trees. The twigs are chewed first to split them into fibres and used as a brush. Also, the bamboo stick is used to make the outlines.

The Family

The same craft has been carried on from generations together. His father is also still practising this craft while he passed on the art to him. Everyone in the family is still practising this craft. There is a specific style that all the motifs follow and each painting has its own name, e.g Panihari, there is a story depicted in the painting, there are instrument players, Ganesha, paniharis, wood lifters, workers, etc then there are astrologers, pujaris, etc. the painting that we were looking at depicted the Mahakali of Pavagadh

Art Journal

They have recently discovered a 300-year-old pachedi (painting). They kept is very safe and they don’t generally take it out. They take it out only on special occasions. Before they also made use of precious metals like gold and silver on the painting.

Handicrafts

They generally don’t repeat the figures. They imagine the design in their minds and then start plotting them on the cloth. Thus, no matter the style remains the same, if he and his father have to make a figure of Ganesh, both of them will make a different representation of Ganesh.
